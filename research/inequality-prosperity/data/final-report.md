# Is Inequality Necessary for Economic Prosperity?

## A final report consolidating empirical evidence, theoretical frameworks, country case studies, agent-based simulation, and a hegemon-confounder robustness test.

---

## The answer in one paragraph

**No. Inequality past a moderate threshold is not necessary for economic prosperity or innovation — and the evidence increasingly suggests it actively suppresses both.** Some dispersion of rewards is necessary to motivate effort, risk-taking, and capital formation. Wealth concentration of the kind seen in the contemporary US is not. The empirical relationship between inequality and growth, across institutional studies (IMF, OECD, World Bank), micro-level innovation research, country case studies, and a rebuilt agent-based simulation, is an **inverted-U**: prosperity peaks at moderate inequality (income Gini ~0.25–0.35), and falls at both extremes. (Note: this is an *income* Gini; the matching *wealth* Gini is far higher — ~0.70 even in the Nordic economies and the model's own innovation optimum, vs ~0.85 in the US.) The popular framing — "inequality is the price we pay for growth" — inverts the empirical relationship past a modest threshold.

---

## Section 1: Sharpening the question

The original framing collapses four distinct concepts that the evidence treats very differently. Most public debate suffers from conflating them.

| Concept | What it means | Evidence verdict |
|---|---|---|
| **Differential rewards** | Higher pay for higher productivity, risk, or scarce skill | Necessary for prosperity |
| **Wealth dispersion** | Some households hold more capital than others | Necessary for risk capital |
| **Wealth concentration** | Top 1% / top 10% holds a large share of total wealth | Harmful past a threshold |
| **Intergenerational lock-in** | Parental wealth strongly predicts children's outcomes | Strongly harmful to innovation |

The pro-inequality argument is strongest for rows 1 and 2; the empirical case against high inequality applies to rows 3 and 4. Treating these as a single dial is the central confusion. **The real question is not "should there be inequality?" but "what kind, of what magnitude, with what mobility?"**

## Section 2: What the theory says

Five theoretical pillars are commonly cited in support of inequality. None survives close inspection as a justification for the levels of concentration currently observed in the US.

**Kaldor's savings hypothesis** — capitalists save ~70% of profits versus ~5% of wages, so concentration funds capital accumulation ([Kaldor 1961](http://gesd.free.fr/kaldor61.pdf)). Mechanically true in early-industrial economies; weakens dramatically in modern economies where corporate retained earnings, pension funds, and foreign capital dominate domestic savings.

**Schumpeterian rents** — entrepreneurs need outsized returns to take risks; creative destruction depends on temporary monopoly profits. True, but Schumpeter's mechanism requires *entry*. High inequality is innovation-friendly when it reflects new entrants displacing incumbents and is innovation-harmful when incumbents entrench themselves. Aghion et al. find that innovation by *entrants* raises top incomes *and* mobility, while innovation by *incumbents* raises top incomes while reducing mobility ([Aghion et al., RES 2019](https://academic.oup.com/restud/article/86/1/1/5026613)).

**Tournament theory** — large prizes at the top elicit effort throughout the hierarchy ([Lazear & Rosen, JPE 1981](https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:ucp:jpolec:v:89:y:1981:i:5:p:841-64)). Justifies meaningful pay differentials but loses force once the tournament loses legitimacy, after which effort collapses.

**Hayekian price signals** — unequal outcomes carry information about scarce skills and preferences; flattening them destroys the signal. Real, but Hayek's argument is for *dispersion*, not *concentration*. The Soviet failure is a vote against suppressing price signals, not for the contemporary US distribution.

**Marginal-productivity defense** — Mankiw argued the top 1% earns its income through marginal productivity, making redistribution morally costly ([Mankiw, JEP 2013](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Fjep.27.3.21)). Increasingly contested empirically: post-2000 top income growth tracks rents in finance, real estate, and platform monopoly far more than measurable productivity.

Five competing frameworks argue the opposite. These are now the institutional consensus among the IMF, OECD, and World Bank since 2010.

**Galor-Zeira credit constraints** — when poor households can't borrow against future earnings, they under-invest in education even when returns exceed the interest rate. Quantified effect: redistribution toward the 25th percentile raises GDP growth by ~2.3 percentage points ([Galor-Zeira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galor%E2%80%93Zeira_model)).

**Bell-Chetty "Lost Einsteins"** — children of top-1% families are *ten times* more likely to file a patent by age 30 than below-median children, controlling for math ability in third grade ([Bell, Chetty et al., QJE 2019](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/134/2/647/5218522)). Equalizing access would quadruple US inventors.

**Summers secular stagnation** — rising inequality raises the average propensity to save, suppressing aggregate demand and creating chronic excess saving over investment. Summers: "lack of demand creates lack of supply potential" ([Summers 2015](http://www.piketty.pse.ens.fr/files/Summers2015.pdf)).

**Acemoglu-Robinson institutional capture** — economic inequality flows into political inequality, which reshapes institutions to be extractive rather than inclusive. Extractive institutions then suppress entry, competition, and innovation ([Why Nations Fail](https://www.wcfia.harvard.edu/publications/why-nations-fail)).

**Great Gatsby Curve** — cross-country, the Gini coefficient explains roughly 65% of the variation in intergenerational earnings elasticity. US intergenerational elasticity is ~0.47; Nordic countries cluster at 0.15–0.18 ([Corak 2013](https://stonecenter.gc.cuny.edu/files/2013/07/corak-income-inequality-equality-of-opportunity-and-intergenerational-mobility-2013.pdf)).

## Section 3: What the cross-country data says

The post-2010 institutional consensus is striking in its uniformity.

| Study | Finding | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **IMF (Ostry, Berg, Tsangarides 2014)** | 3-point Gini rise reduces annual growth by ~0.5 pp; redistribution is "generally benign" | [IMF SDN 2014](https://www.imf.org/-/media/websites/imf/imported-full-text-pdf/external/pubs/ft/sdn/2014/_sdn1402.pdf) |
| **OECD (Cingano 2014)** | Average Gini rise across OECD members cost ~8.5% of cumulative GDP over 25 years | [OECD report](https://www.komazawa-u.ac.jp/~kobamasa/reference/bibliography/OECD/Focus-Inequality-and-Growth-2014.pdf) |
| **Berg & Ostry (IMF 2011)** | Lower inequality robustly predicts *longer* growth spells | [IMF F&D](https://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/fandd/2014/09/pdf/ostry.pdf) |
| **Banerjee & Duflo (JEG 2003)** | *Changes* in inequality in either direction reduce growth — non-parametric inverted-U | [MIT working paper](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/2022-08/Inequality%20and%20Growth%20What%20Can%20the%20Data%20Say.pdf) |
| **Alesina & Rodrik (QJE 1994)** | Distributive conflict from high inequality reduces growth | [NBER 3668](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w3668/w3668.pdf) |
| **Forbes (AER 2000)** | Positive short-run effect over 5-year periods — the main heterodox finding; driven heavily by post-Soviet transition countries | [AER 2000](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Faer.90.4.869) |
| **Barro (2000)** | Inequality harmful in poor countries, neutral/positive in rich ones, with a threshold around $11,900 GDP/capita | [Barro paper](https://barro.scholars.harvard.edu/file_url/293) |

The IMF's 2014 finding is the single most important update to economic thinking on this question. It directly overturned Okun's "leaky bucket" intuition that redistribution must trade off against growth.

## Section 4: What the country cases show

Seven natural experiments converge on the inverted-U.

**Nordic model.** Gini 0.27–0.28, R&D spending 3.0–3.6% of GDP, Global Innovation Index ranks of 2, 7, 9, and 20 ([IZA DP 17677](https://repec.iza.org/dp17677.pdf)). Equality achieved through *predistribution* (coordinated wage bargaining), not just redistribution. Labor productivity per hour matches or exceeds the US.

**East Asian Tigers.** South Korea and Taiwan grew 6–9% per year for three decades at Gini 0.30–0.35. Taiwan's pre-industrial land reform compressed its Gini from 0.57 to 0.33 *before* industrializing. South Korea now ranks 4th globally on innovation with R&D near 5% of GDP — the highest in the world.

**Latin America (Brazil, Mexico, Colombia).** Gini 0.44–0.52, R&D 0.29–1.15% of GDP, GII ranks 49–65. Labor productivity grew 0.2% per year from 1980–2008 ([CGDev](https://www.cgdev.org/publication/latin-americas-hard-to-escape-stagnation-trap)). High inequality, persistent stagnation.

**Post-war US "Great Compression" (1945–1973).** Top 1% share averaged 8–10%, TFP growth ~2.88% per year. The post-1980 era saw top 1% share more than double to ~23.5% while productivity growth fell to 0.8% per year. *Suggestive but confounded by hegemon status — see Section 6.*

**China post-1978.** Gini rose from 0.30 to ~0.47 alongside 9.4% annual GDP growth. The inequality was *policy-engineered* via the hukou system; the early growth (1978–1990) occurred at much lower inequality. Inequality accompanied growth but did not cause it.

**Soviet Union / Cuba.** Gini ~0.28; TFP fell from 2.8% per year to effectively negative. The failure mechanism was not equality but *coercive outcome suppression* combined with the absence of price signals and the lack of firm entry/exit. The Nordics achieve similar Ginis through wage compression *within* a market economy, with opposite innovation outcomes.

**US Gilded Age (1870–1900).** Top 10% owned ~75% of wealth alongside enormous patent activity. Two confounders: real wages grew 60% across the period (broad-based gains alongside concentration), and growth was extremely volatile, averaging only 1.78% per year. Public infrastructure (land grants, patent system, public universities) underpinned private innovation.

## Section 5: The simulation

> **Note (2026 rebuild).** The simulation originally reported here was rebuilt from scratch after an audit found three disqualifying bugs: innovation was normalized by the population mean (pinning the innovation count constant in every regime — the old "innovation is flat across regimes, 2,924–3,051" result was an artifact, not a finding); the credit constraint was an absolute threshold that became vacuous once incomes compounded; and taxes were modeled as pure consumption transfer, so "redistribution lowers growth" was true by construction. The old six-regime table and 18-point Gini sweep (including the "growth peaks at Gini 0.45–0.55" claim) are **withdrawn**. What follows is the rebuilt model and its results.

A simulation cannot prove what is true of real economies. Its one unique power is **decoupling**: in real data, four things always move together — reward dispersion (what an innovator personally gains), standing wealth concentration, what taxes fund, and access to finance. A model can vary them independently. So "is inequality necessary for innovation?" is split into three sub-claims tested *inside* the model:

- **H1 — incentive necessity:** does innovation collapse when reward dispersion ρ → 0? (Near-tautological in any incentive model; stated, not claimed as a discovery.)
- **H2 — concentration necessity:** holding rewards fixed, does *standing wealth concentration* raise innovation (risk capital, project scale-up) or lower it (talented poor excluded)?
- **H3 — substitutability:** does concentration's financing role survive credit markets and tax-funded public R&D as alternatives?

The model (1,000 agents, 200 periods, 30 seeds per configuration, reproducible) gives each agent a **talent** drawn independently of wealth — talent is distributed equally; opportunity is not. Innovation projects cost a scale-free amount and can be financed three ways: self-finance (the only route that permits concave *scale-up* — the genuine pro-concentration channel), limited-liability credit, or tax-funded public R&D with a co-pay. Education is scale-free (Baumol) and gated by a relative poverty line that public education bypasses. Rents to successful innovators are paid as a capped markup, so successful innovators get rich — inequality is partly an *outcome* of innovation here, not just an input. Calibration constants are frozen from a neutral baseline (median human capital held stationary), never tuned toward any inequality-growth shape; the US-like baseline passes validation gates (reproduces wealth Gini ≈ 0.73, income Gini < wealth Gini, an interior poverty gate, sane growth). The contested demand-side channel from the old model is *excluded*.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Three results, each stated only where the 95% confidence intervals separate:

1. **H1 holds, trivially.** With ρ = 0 (no reward to innovation) the innovation count is zero at every τ. Differential reward is necessary — but this is built into any incentive model and is not evidence about how *much* dispersion is needed.
2. **Laissez-faire is never innovation-optimal (H2).** For every positive reward level, innovation is an inverted-U in redistribution and the peak is *interior* — τ\* between 0.2 and 0.6, never 0. At τ = 0 (wealth Gini ≈ 0.89), **98–99% of the most talented agents never innovate** — they cannot afford the education gate. Standing wealth concentration does not buy innovation; it wastes inventor talent. The model's own "lost Einsteins" count is the Bell-Chetty mechanism made endogenous.
3. **The optimum is at high reward but moderate inequality.** The innovation-optimal *income* Gini lands at ≈ 0.24–0.31 — the Nordic range — while the *wealth* Gini stays high (≈ 0.70–0.76), which is also true of real Nordic economies. The required ingredient is reward dispersion (a flow), not wealth concentration (a stock).

### 5.2 Why redistribution helps here — and where it stops helping

The inverted-U has a mechanism on each arm, isolated by switching channels off one at a time:

| Channel removed | What happens to innovation-vs-τ |
|---|---|
| **Public R&D** (taxes no longer fund research) | The entire upslope vanishes — innovation collapses monotonically with τ. This is the old broken model's assumption, and it reproduces the old spurious "redistribution hurts" result. |
| **Incentive gate** (innovators attempt regardless of after-tax payoff) | The downslope vanishes — innovation rises monotonically with τ. |
| **Credit access / project scale-up** (the pro-*concentration* channels) | Almost no effect. In this calibration, concentration-as-risk-capital is weak. |

So the **upslope** (τ from 0 to ≈ 0.3) is public investment — taxes fund education and R&D that unblock talented-but-poor inventors: as τ rises to its optimum the share of agents too poor to attempt falls from 36% to 2%, and lost Einsteins fall from 99% to 51%. The **downslope** (τ beyond the optimum) is incentive erosion — past τ\*, the after-tax reward no longer justifies the effort and *able* innovators stop trying (the incentive-blocked share rises above 90%). Redistribution raises innovation **only to the extent it funds public investment**: a robustness sweep over revenue splits (×3 financing regimes × 3 splits) confirms the interior peak everywhere, but a transfers-heavy split (90% cash transfers) yields the *weakest* innovation, while an R&D-heavy split yields the strongest and peaks at low τ. Credit-market depth barely moves the result.

The simulation is a stylized mechanism map, not evidence about real economies; it bakes in Galor-Zeira credit constraints and a Bell-Chetty talent-exposure channel. What it *adds* to the empirical case is a clean separation: it shows that "inequality is necessary for innovation" conflates a true claim (reward dispersion matters) with a false one (standing wealth concentration helps), and that the entire benefit of redistribution runs through what the revenue funds.

## Section 6: The hegemon-tailwind robustness test

A critical confounder challenged the original analysis: post-war US prosperity is overdetermined by the dollar's reserve status, the rest of the industrial world being bombed flat, Bretton Woods, pent-up demand, and the GI Bill. Attributing the 1945–1973 productivity boom to compressed inequality alone is a leap. The simulation was extended with an exogenous "hegemon" productivity bonus, and three additional country cases were researched.

### 6.1 The simulation extension

> **Note (2026 rebuild).** This hegemon extension runs on the *original* simulation, which has since been withdrawn and rebuilt (see the note opening Section 5). The qualitative point below — that a finite tailwind compounds for decades — does not depend on the buggy innovation/transfer mechanics, but the specific magnitudes here are pending a rebuild of this extension and should be read as illustrative.

Three scenarios were tested per redistribution regime: no hegemon bonus, hegemon bonus during periods 50–100 only, and hegemon bonus active throughout. **A finite tailwind compounds for decades after it ends** — median income trajectories diverged by 10+ orders of magnitude between hegemon and no-hegemon scenarios, with the divergence persisting long after the tailwind window closed. Even a temporary positional advantage gets locked in via compounding.

The inverted-U became weaker in the v2 simulation, revealing that the original simulation's strong inverted-U depended heavily on calibration of the demand-side channel — exactly the most contested mechanism in the literature.

Three economies were studied that also received post-war tailwinds but ran different inequality experiments after the tailwind faded.

**United Kingdom — the prior hegemon.** Sterling held >80% of world FX reserves in 1945 and <10% by the mid-1970s. The UK ran two consecutive experiments: low inequality 1945–1979 under the Attlee welfare state (final-income Gini fell from 37 to 14), then high inequality 1979–present under Thatcher (top 1% share nearly doubled from ~7% to 14.7%). Productivity growth fell across both eras and collapsed entirely after 2008 — UK productivity is now 28% below US, 13% below France, 14% below Germany per hour worked. **The high-inequality era did not deliver the promised productivity gains.** Germany and France built *larger* welfare states than the UK and outgrew it throughout the post-war period. The welfare state did not cause the British disease; weak competition policy and chronic underinvestment did.

**Japan — necessary but not sufficient.** Japan maintained Gini ~0.27–0.32 for forty years, then stagnated for thirty. The Lost Decades are principally explained by balance-sheet recession, policy mismanagement, demographics, and zombie-firm forbearance — not by inequality regime. Japan demonstrates that **low inequality is necessary but not sufficient** for sustained prosperity. You also need functioning corporate restructuring, intact financial intermediation, demographic renewal, and innovation-absorption capacity. The Nordics have all four; Japan has only some.

**Germany — the cleanest demand-side test.** Germany maintained Gini ~0.25–0.27 through 1990 via co-determination, works councils, and sectoral wage bargaining. The Hartz reforms (2003–2005) deliberately raised inequality — Gini rose from 0.262 to 0.288, the fastest postwar increase. Result: a 6–7% of GDP current account surplus, near-zero domestic demand growth, and a structurally underinvested economy with public net investment negative since 1988. **The correlation between Germany's top-decile income share and its current account surplus since 2000 is 0.94.** Germany is the clean demonstration of the Summers/Stiglitz demand-suppression mechanism observed in a natural experiment, not a model.

### 6.3 What the hegemon test changed

| Original claim | Updated claim |
|---|---|
| Post-war US proves low inequality drives prosperity | Post-war US is too confounded to prove anything alone; the cross-country regressions and the East Asian/Nordic/Germany cases are stronger evidence |
| Inverted-U is robust at all calibrations | Inverted-U is robust empirically; the simulation's specific shape depends on demand-side calibration |
| Inequality causes growth slowdowns | Inequality *concentrates* the cost of growth slowdowns onto specific parts of the income distribution |
| Low inequality is the prescription | Low inequality is *necessary but not sufficient*; corporate restructuring, demographics, and institutions must also work |

The central conclusion survives. Moderate inequality outperforms both extremes. Innovation depends more on the breadth of the talent pool than the size of the prize at the top. But the cleaner statement is now:

> **Wealth concentration past a moderate threshold does not buy more growth or innovation. It buys the ability to redirect output toward the top when the macroeconomic tailwind fades — which is a political outcome, not an economic necessity.**

## Section 7: Implications

Stripping away the "inequality is necessary for innovation" justification reveals what high inequality actually delivers.

**For the top decile, high inequality buys:**
- A larger share of a constant or shrinking pie. Top 1% income share roughly doubled in the US, UK, and Germany after their respective inflection points without any corresponding doubling of growth.
- Political influence sufficient to lock in the arrangement.
- Insulation from the post-tailwind productivity slowdown that everyone else absorbed.

**For everyone else, high inequality buys:**
- The Great Gatsby Curve — parental wealth predicts life outcomes more than at any point since the Gilded Age.
- The Lost Einsteins problem — for every kid in the top 1% who becomes an inventor, ten kids with equal third-grade math ability don't.
- Health, longevity, and stress costs that don't show up in GDP.
- Higher household debt as suppressed wages get backfilled by borrowing — the dynamic that preceded 2008.

**For the political system, high inequality buys:**
- Reduced legitimacy of market outcomes. Once people stop believing the tournament is fair, they stop playing — or vote for politicians who promise to overturn it.
- A specific kind of populism on both left and right that targets institutions rather than just policies.

Three honest implications follow from the evidence chain.

**The "growth versus equality" framing is a category error.** It dominated US economic policy from roughly 1980–2010, built on Okun's "leaky bucket." The IMF, OECD, and World Bank have all walked back from that position post-2010. The "we have to tolerate inequality for prosperity" argument is no longer the institutional consensus among the institutions that historically made it.

**The bottleneck for innovation in advanced economies is not a shortage of rich people willing to fund risky bets — it is a shortage of pathways for talent to reach the frontier.** The Bell-Chetty Lost Einsteins finding is the most policy-actionable result in modern labor economics. Expanding childcare, K-12 investment, debt-free higher education for high-return fields, geographic mobility support, and direct R&D funding deliver higher returns than tax cuts on capital gains.

**The political stability question may matter more than the growth question.** Even granting the most generous pro-inequality reading — that current US inequality costs only ~0.5 percentage points of annual growth — the political instability cost likely exceeds the growth cost. Highly unequal democracies in the historical record either reform (UK 1945, post-Depression US) or destabilize (interwar Europe, much of Latin America). The US is 45 years into "stay highly unequal and don't reform," and the political consequences are increasingly visible.

## Section 8: What a designed system would look like

If the goal were maximum innovation and prosperity, the evidence points to a specific blueprint. Not a guaranteed recipe — no country has built exactly this — but a defensible inference from the convergent findings.

**Layer 0 (load-bearing constraints):** Markets remain the price-discovery mechanism. Property rights are real and predictable. Rule of law is symmetric across the income distribution. Information flows (statistics, courts, press) are independent. Without these, no inequality-tuning fixes anything.

**Layer 1 (maximize the inventor pool — the highest-return lever):** Universal early childhood. Debt-free pathways through high-return fields (STEM, medicine, skilled trades). Inventor-exposure programs operationalizing Bell-Chetty (research apprenticeships, mentorship at scale). Geographic mobility support. Ruthlessly accessible high-skill immigration.

**Layer 2 (keep capital flowing to entry, not entrenchment):** Aggressive antitrust against incumbent dominance. Tax structure distinguishing capital *formation* (low taxes on founder equity, R&D, long-held capital gains) from capital *concentration* (higher taxes on inherited wealth above modest exemption, short-term gains, carried interest, monopoly rents, share buybacks beyond a threshold). Public co-investment in hard tech where private VC underprovides. Bankruptcy that lets failed entrepreneurs start again.

**Layer 3 (redistribute the floor, not the ceiling):** Refundable EITC, child allowance, housing voucher, food assistance. Universal healthcare delinked from employment — also an entrepreneurship unlock. Wage floor indexed to median wage growth. Sectoral wage bargaining where it works, while explicitly avoiding the Hartz overshoot that creates suppressed-demand traps. Portable benefits that follow the worker.

**Layer 4 (institutional honesty — what Japan revealed is necessary but not sufficient):** Functioning corporate restructuring; zombie firms must die. Counter-cyclical macro stabilization. Stable monetary regime with central bank independence. Anti-corruption that bites. Demographic renewal through immigration and family policy.

**Layer 5 (the open frontier):** Direct state R&D funding for foundational research (Mazzucato's entrepreneurial state thesis applies to basic research; less clear for applied). Industrial policy tied to public-goods problems (the IRA is the live test). AI governance to determine whether productivity gains diffuse or concentrate.

**What this system plausibly delivers:** Gini around 0.28–0.32 (current Nordic range) as a consequence rather than a target. Inventor density 2–3× current US per capita. Productivity growth 1.5–2.5% per year. Intergenerational mobility 2–3× current US level. Lower volatility.

**What it doesn't deliver:** Gilded Age billionaire concentration. Maximum short-term equity returns. Pure laissez-faire libertarian ideals.

**How it fails:** Capture by elites if anti-corruption doesn't bite. Stagnation if banking or demographics break (the Japan trap). Political durability is harder at US scale and heterogeneity — federalism is probably the answer. External shocks (energy, war, pandemic) can wreck any domestic design. The blueprint assumes you're already a frontier economy; catch-up economies tolerate more concentration.

## Section 9: Final answer

**Is inequality necessary for economic prosperity?**

Some dispersion of rewards is necessary. The high concentration observed in the contemporary US is not. The evidence — from cross-country econometrics, micro-level innovation research, seven country case studies, an agent-based simulation, and a hegemon-confounder robustness test — converges on an inverted-U with a sweet spot near Gini 0.25–0.35.

Past that threshold, additional inequality does not buy growth. It does not buy innovation. It buys distributional outcomes: who absorbs the productivity slowdown, who gets to invent, whose children inherit the future. Those are political choices dressed up as economic necessities.

The strongest piece of evidence is the Bell-Chetty Lost Einsteins finding: the US has *quadrupled-fewer inventors* than it could have, because the inventor pipeline runs through wealthy zip codes. That is a self-imposed innovation tax, paid in foregone discoveries. No other developed country pays it at the same rate.

The strongest piece of mechanism evidence is Germany's Hartz experiment: a deliberate, recent, well-instrumented natural experiment showing that raising bottom-end inequality suppresses domestic demand and produces a current account surplus rather than higher productivity growth. The correlation between top-decile income share and current account surplus is 0.94. The Summers/Stiglitz demand-suppression mechanism is observed, not merely theorized.

The strongest piece of counter-evidence is Japan: low inequality is necessary but not sufficient. Without functioning corporate restructuring, demographic renewal, and innovation-absorption capacity, a low-Gini economy can stagnate for decades. The Nordics succeed because they have all the supporting institutions; Japan has only some.

The strongest hedge against overconfidence is the Soviet case: coercive equalization without intact markets destroys innovation. The mechanism that matters is whether prices, entry, exit, and risk-taking remain intact. The Gini number is a downstream consequence of those choices, not the lever itself.

**The bottom line for policy and for politics: the question is not whether to tolerate inequality for prosperity. The question is who absorbs the productivity slowdown that's happening regardless, and whether the inventor pool stays narrow or broadens. That is a political choice, and the evidence shows the broader pool produces more prosperity, not less.**
